The organs

Definition:

Equine anatomy encompasses the gross and microscopic anatomy of horses, ponies and other equids, including donkeys, mules and zebras. While all anatomical features of equids are described in the same terms as for other animals by the International Committee on Veterinary Gross Anatomical Nomenclature in the book Nomina Anatomica Veterinaria, there are many horse-specific colloquial terms used by equestrians.

The forehand

The forehand is the part of the horse that is in front of the rider when in the saddle. The forehand consists of the head, neck, shoulders and forelimbs. She receives the indications of the superior helpers and gives the direction of the movement. Raising the forehand contributes to the balance of the school and fighting horse. It is inseparable from the lowering of the hindquarters of which it is the consequence. Its extension causes contrary to the lengthening of gaits and the displacement of the center of gravity of horse and rider forward.


The head


The horse's head presents few morphological variations, probably due to of the herbivorous diet and the digestion process1. In particular, the teeth must be positioned in such a way as to allow for the horse to graze on plants on the ground1. Another particularity of the facial morphology of the horse is the role that it plays in matter of intraspecific communication.

The head profil


A point of morphological differentiation in the horse's head is its profile, which can be convex (synonym: curved), rectilinear, or concave1 (synonym: camus5). The majority of horses present a profile of straight head, or slightly concave1. Convex profiles are rarer, and typically associated with Iberian horses and other baroque frames1. The Arab is renowned for its head profile concave, while the Shire most often has a head profile convex. The term "subconvex" designates a head profile with a minor convexity, which becomes more evident at the nose1. Certain horses, often ponies and breeds from northern countries, have a head that is concave at the forehead and convex in its lower part, giving an “S” shaped head profile6. We speaks of a “rhinoceros head”, if the concavity is limited to the chamfer5, and “mutton head” when only the chamfer is convex. The “hare`s head” has a convexity limited to the part frontal

Middle part

Body The body is the part of the horse that lies under the rider when he is in the saddle. The body consists of: the withers, back, loins, chest, ars, inter-ars, ribs, passage of strap, flanks, stomach, inguinal region, the anus, perineum, coward, and tail; of the genital tract of the horse: the vulva and the udders for the mare, the penis, the purses and sheath for the stallion.

Hindquarters

Hindquarters The hindquarters are the part of the horse located behind the saddle. It is composed 1,c 1: loins, rump, hips; of the hind limbs: thigh, buttock, fold of the buttocks, tip of the buttocks, stifle, stifle fold, leg, kneecap, hock, cord of hock, knuckle bend, hock point, hock hollow, cannon, tendons, chestnut, fetlock, dewclaws and their dewlaps, pastern, fold of pastern, crown, heel, foot, hoof. The crushed hindquarters causes the cornering, the encamped hindquarters prohibits the retreat, the hindquarters suitably lowered and bent allows it to be gathered together and the relaxation of the springs in the forward movement. The hindquarters lightweight allows maximum galloping speed.

The hoof

The foot constitutes the end of the horse's limbs. The other members of the genus Equus, donkey and zebra, have the same foot morphology which distinguishes them from their equine ancestors. It is an essential element of locomotion which fully justifies the expression “no foot, no horse”. The horse is a perissodactyl, with an odd number of toes - in this case only one - whose body weight is supported by the middle toe. In comparative anatomy, the horse's foot therefore corresponds to the last two phalanges of the middle finger of the human species.
